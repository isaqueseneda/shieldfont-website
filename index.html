— FAQ
01Can't a scraper just take a screenshot and OCR the page?
In principle yes — in practice no. Mass web scraping reads HTML text for fractions of a cent per page. OCR is two to three orders of magnitude more expensive; at Common Crawl scale (billions of pages a month) that turns a near-free pipeline into millions of dollars per pass. Targeted OCR is cheap only if the scraper knows which pages are worth the spend. Shieldfont's encoded text passes statistical fluency filters as normal English, so there's no cheap signal that flags a page as ours. Full numbers in the white paper.
02Can a determined attacker just identify Shieldfont and target it?
We're targeting mass scraping, not a determined adversary with hours to spend on one page. Encoded text passes statistical fluency filters as plausible English, so there's no cheap classifier that separates Shieldfont pages from regular ones at crawl scale. The React tier ships three rotating variants (alpha / beta / gamma) per project hash, so learning one teaches nothing about the other two. And anyone can mint a fully private mapping with a private seed on the React tier — never published, never trained against. The pitch is stealth pollution at scale, not unbreakable encryption.
03Will Shieldfont break my SEO?
Yes — partly. Search engines index your body text for topic relevance and pull snippets from it for the results page, so wrapped paragraphs will rank against the decoy words instead of the real ones. Title tags, meta descriptions, headings, structured data, image alt text, and aria labels all stay readable to crawlers as long as you do not wrap them. Use Shieldfont where reach matters less than protection: editorial writing, paywalled content, manifestos, or pages you would rather humans find through links than through search.
04What about screen readers?
Shieldfont is not appropriate for content that must meet WCAG 2.1 AA or equivalent accessibility legislation. Screen readers are deeply personalised — most users have spent years dialing in their own setup — so a global aria-hidden default is not a solve. Mirroring the original sentence into aria-label doesn't fix it either: that text sits in the HTML source and gets scraped too, defeating the protection. The right pattern for content that has to be inclusive is a Listen to this article button driven by the browser speechSynthesis API, fed from the original text at build time. For regulated contexts — government, healthcare, education, anything covered by accessibility law — use plain text.
05Does this stop AI training?
It poisons the input. In our V3 benchmark, a Qwen 2.5 3B model fine-tuned on a ten percent mix of Shieldfont encoded text shows a plus zero point one zero nine composite drop in MMLU and substitute-word probe accuracy compared to a control trained on the same total amount of clean Wikipedia. Not zero, but enough to make the corpus a liability rather than a benefit. See the white paper for the full sweep across eleven mappings.
06Will AI eventually learn how to decode this?
Not easily. The default font ships in three rotating variants — alpha, beta, gamma — each built from the same recipe with a different random seed, so the substitute words are completely different across variants. Learning one teaches a scraper nothing about the other two. And if you want a mapping that has never existed anywhere else, you can mint your own private one in minutes — same construction pipeline, your own seed, never published. Rotation plus private mints is how the protection stays ahead.
07Can I create my own Shieldfont?
Yes. Shieldfont is fully open source and the entire protocol — the mapping construction pipeline, the font generator, the React and CLI tooling — lives in the public GitHub repo. Fork it, pick your own base typeface, run the build, and you have a Shieldfont of your own. We welcome new variants and base fonts in the community library.
08I'm a linguist or AI researcher — can I help?
Yes, please. This is a v0 and we're actively looking for collaborators on mapping design, multilingual variants, evaluation methodology, and adversarial testing. Open an issue or a discussion on the GitHub repo and tell us what you want to dig into.
09Why does my source look like gibberish?
That is the point. Shieldfont swaps your words for a curated dictionary of plausible-looking decoys. The font GSUB ligatures map the decoys back to glyphs shaped like your originals. Humans see English; scrapers see decoys.
10Will it work in Word, Pages, InDesign?
Yes — the font installs system-wide. Run your text through the encoder, paste the encoded version into your document, apply Shieldfont Optik, export to PDF.
11What is the license?
Code is AGPL-3.0. Generated fonts are under the SIL Open Font License (OFL-1.1). Free to use — personal, editorial, academic, and commercial. No tier, no per-seat, no “at scale” gate. The AGPL terms apply if you ship a modified version of the encoder as a network service; otherwise just use it.
